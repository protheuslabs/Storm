# Platform Concepts

This document captures larger Storm concepts that came from earlier source material. These ideas are not all MVP features, but they may influence the long-term domain model.

## Idea

An idea is an early-stage concept, prompt, problem, or goal that may become a project.

Unlike a project, an idea may not yet have:

- A budget.
- A task list.
- An owner.
- A timeline.
- Clear acceptance criteria.

Future Storm may let users contribute to ideas before they become funded projects.

## Creative Seed

A creative seed is an idea captured early enough that the system can preserve it, grow it, and attribute later value back to the people who helped it develop.

The old notes frame creative seeds like investments:

- A person contributes an idea or useful creative input.
- Others build on it.
- Storm tracks provenance.
- Downstream value can flow back to upstream contributors.

Creative seeds are future scope. The first MVP starts with funded projects and direct task payouts.

## Container

A container is a nested unit of meaning or work.

Examples:

- Idea.
- Goal.
- Problem.
- What-if.
- Part.
- Task.
- Microtask.

The container concept matters because the old Storm vision is not only task management. It is recursive decomposition from vague goals into executable units.

## Microtask

A microtask is a very small task that can be completed quickly with minimal context.

The old source describes microtasks as tasks that may take a minute or a few minutes, potentially on a phone. In practice, the MVP should use larger tasks first because review, payout, and context costs can overwhelm extremely small work units.

## Contribution

A contribution is any positive action that helps create value.

Examples:

- Creating an idea.
- Clarifying a problem.
- Breaking work into smaller containers.
- Solving a task.
- Reviewing a submission.
- Providing useful research.
- Funding a project.
- Resolving a dispute.

The MVP starts with task submissions and reviews. Later versions may track more contribution types.

## Project Applet

A project applet is a focused project-type surface built on the Storm core.

The applet owns domain-specific metadata, task templates, artifact types, review criteria, merge flows, and export formats. Storm core owns generic coordination, review, reputation, ledger, and provenance.

Examples:

- Story Forge.
- Code Forge.
- Research Forge.
- Invention Forge.

## Residual Claim

A residual claim is a continuing right to value generated by later work that builds on an earlier contribution.

This is central to the old Storm vision but should not be part of the first paid MVP until the rules are precise.

Open design issues:

- How contribution weight is calculated.
- How long residuals last.
- How downstream dilution works.
- How legacy ideas are handled.
- How disputes over original contribution are resolved.
- How legal ownership maps to platform accounting.

## AI Training Provenance

AI training provenance is the record of which source contributions were included in datasets, training runs, model contexts, AI outputs, or downstream AI value events.

The principle is that AI may be allowed to train on Storm data, and later approved data from the Protheus Labs ecosystem, but training access must not erase origin. If value is generated from traceable source work, the originators should be eligible for compensation.

This is future scope, but it should influence how contribution events, artifacts, and datasets are modeled.

## Contribution Stewardship

Contribution stewardship is the idea that the people who contribute to a project should have enforceable attribution and value rights without necessarily holding traditional exclusionary ownership.

In Storm, ownership should not be the deepest primitive. Provenance, usage rights, governance rules, and value routing matter more.

## Non-Withholding Legal Wrapper

A non-withholding legal wrapper is a patent, business, license, contract, or entity structure that can interact with existing law while preserving Storm's contribution and value-routing rules.

The purpose of the wrapper is not to let a legal owner capture value. It is to make sure external legal systems cannot erase contributor rights.

## Protheus Network Node

A future Protheus network node is a user-owned runtime participant that can hold local authority, produce receipts, validate incoming signals, and participate in network coordination.

Storm may eventually use Protheus/InfRing primitives when a single InfRing instance can become a network node.

Versioning and fork lineage may also belong in the Protheus/InfRing substrate. Storm can expose GitHub-inspired product workflows, but a shared primitive would let projects fork, merge, replicate, and preserve provenance across applets and nodes.

## Group

A group is a persistent collaboration unit. It may be closer to a community, team, company, guild, or decentralized organization depending on its rules.

Possible group capabilities:

- Recruit members.
- Own projects.
- Maintain member roles.
- Choose governance rules.
- Seed child groups or projects.
- Split value among members.
- Maintain internal reputation.

## Group Template

A group template is a reusable organizational pattern.

Future templates may define:

- Default roles.
- Governance rules.
- Recruiting flows.
- Internal divisions.
- Decision rights.
- Payout and reserve rules.
- Growth-stage transitions.

The old notes suggest groups may evolve as they grow. Templates are a practical way to test that idea without making every group fully custom from day one.

## Group Lifecycle

A group lifecycle is a staged model for how a group changes as it grows.

Possible lifecycle triggers:

- Member count.
- Revenue.
- Age.
- Project count.
- Available resources.
- Competitive pressure.

Lifecycle stages are future scope. They should not be implemented until normal group ownership and governance work.

## Child Group

A child group is a group seeded by an existing parent group.

Possible uses:

- Spin off a focused project.
- Start a domain-specific alliance.
- Let successful operating patterns reproduce.
- Allocate residual value back to the parent group.

Child-group royalties are a residual compensation problem and should remain outside MVP.

## Alliance

An alliance is a larger or more durable group structure that can coordinate around a problem space, project category, or public need.

Alliances may eventually emerge from:

- Trending problems.
- Popular suggestions.
- Preorders or pledged funding.
- Volunteer commitments.
- Strong contributors in a domain.

Alliance governance should make leadership accountable. The old notes emphasize that official leaders should be removable and that real leadership may emerge informally through merit.

## Public Pledge

A public pledge is a signal that a user wants an idea, problem, or future project to exist.

Pledge types may include:

- Funding pledge.
- Preorder.
- Volunteer offer.
- Research offer.
- Interest signal.

Pledges are useful before a project exists. They help Storm detect demand and form alliances or projects around problems that have enough support.

## Builder

Builder is a future execution environment for creating and upgrading projects.

Near-term Builder can mean:

- Submission links.
- Integration with external tools.
- Artifact tracking.
- Invitations for strong contributors to join future upgrades.

Long-term Builder may include Storm-native creation tools. That is a product line decision, not an MVP dependency.

## World Eye

World Eye is a future crowdsourced intelligence layer.

It would collect and verify information about real-world problems, events, and trends. This information could then feed new projects, tasks, bounties, or research.

This is a separate product surface from the MVP, but its concepts overlap with reputation, veracity scoring, source tracking, and bounties.

## Bolt

Bolt is a future real-world task bounty layer.

It would route physical-world tasks to qualified users based on skill, trust, location, and importance.

This should be treated as a later product line because it involves safety, liability, emergency handling, identity, location, and regulatory concerns.

## Karma

The old source mentions Karma as a currency-like reputation or value system.

Near-term Storm should avoid treating reputation as currency. Reputation should first be evidence of verified behavior. If a currency-like system is explored later, it should be separated from the basic trust score and evaluated carefully.

## Build Order Implication

Storm can preserve the long-term vision without building all of it immediately by layering concepts:

1. Tasks and one-time payouts.
2. Projects and transparent ledgers.
3. Contribution events beyond task completion.
4. Groups and governance.
5. Group templates, alliances, and public pledges.
6. Ideas, creative seeds, and recursive containers.
7. AI training provenance.
8. Non-withholding legal wrappers.
9. Protheus/InfRing network substrate.
10. Residual compensation.
11. Intelligence and real-world bounty networks.
