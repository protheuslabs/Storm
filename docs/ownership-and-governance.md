# Ownership And Governance

Storm mixes market incentives with commons-oriented ownership.

It is not pure capitalism because ownership should not give a person or entity unlimited power to withhold value created by a crowd. It is not pure communism because individual contribution, value creation, skill, risk, and effort still matter and should be compensated.

Storm's proposed answer is contribution-based value routing: preserve incentives and individual agency while preventing ownership or central authority from becoming a permanent extraction layer.

Storm's model is closer to contribution-based stewardship:

- Everyone who contributes has a claim.
- No one should have absolute exclusionary control.
- Value should flow according to contribution, provenance, review, risk, and downstream usefulness.
- Legal wrappers may exist, but they should not become extraction chokepoints.

## Ownership Is Not The Primitive

Traditional systems ask:

> Who owns this?

Storm should ask:

> Who contributed to this, what rights do they have, who may use it, and how does value flow when it creates downstream value?

Ownership may still exist legally because current systems require ownership records, patent holders, business entities, contracts, and liability structures. But inside Storm, ownership should be treated as a legal interface, not the deepest truth of the system.

The deeper truth is provenance plus value routing.

## Shared Ownership Without Deadlock

The phrase "everyone who contributes owns it, and yet no one does" should be interpreted carefully.

Storm should avoid two failure modes:

- Traditional ownership: a small group owns the asset and extracts value from everyone else.
- Collective deadlock: too many contributors have veto power and the work cannot move.

The desired model:

- Contributors have enforceable attribution and value rights.
- No contributor can arbitrarily block legitimate downstream use.
- No legal owner can withhold value from contributors.
- Governance rules decide when work can fork, merge, license, sell, or spin out.

## Legal Wrappers

Storm may produce:

- Patents.
- Businesses.
- Product entities.
- Licenses.
- Revenue contracts.
- Data licenses.
- Research outputs.

These wrappers may be necessary to interact with the outside world. But they should be legally structured so that:

- Contributor rights survive incorporation.
- Revenue cannot be hidden from the ledger.
- Patents cannot be used to erase contributors.
- A business cannot capture crowd-created value without routing value back.
- Forks and spinouts preserve upstream provenance.

## Non-Withholding Principle

Storm should prevent parties from withholding value that was created by the network.

Examples:

- A project owner cannot take approved work and refuse payout.
- A business formed from Storm work cannot ignore upstream contributor claims.
- A patent holder cannot use legal title to erase the contribution graph.
- An AI system trained on Storm work cannot strip source provenance.
- A group cannot fork a project and deny upstream value flow.

Forking should work more like open source lineage than corporate escape. A fork may create independence, but it should not create amnesia. Upstream provenance, applicable value policies, licenses, legal wrappers, and contribution rights must travel with the lineage unless governance explicitly changes them through visible rules.

## Trust Requirements

This model only works if the system is highly trustworthy.

Storm needs:

- Append-oriented contribution records.
- Transparent value ledger.
- Verifiable review and approval events.
- Clear license and usage terms.
- Originality and rights checks before risky work is merged or monetized.
- Dispute resolution.
- Provenance-preserving artifacts.
- Governance rules visible before contribution.
- Audit trails for legal wrappers, patents, businesses, and AI datasets.
- Strong resistance to collusion, fake contribution, and provenance stripping.

## Economic Character

Storm uses market mechanics where they are useful:

- Bounties.
- Payouts.
- Pricing.
- Demand signals.
- Contribution-weighted rewards.
- Reputation.
- Competition between approaches.

Storm uses commons mechanics where they are useful:

- Shared project building.
- Collective contribution graphs.
- Open participation.
- Reusable knowledge.
- Non-extractive access.
- Governance over shared resources.

The goal is not to pick capitalism or communism. The goal is to build a value system where incentives remain strong but ownership cannot become a tool for value capture by a narrow intermediary.

## Product Implication

The MVP should not attempt to solve all legal ownership.

It should start with:

- Clear task terms.
- Clear payout rules.
- Contribution records.
- Project ledger.
- Review history.
- Basic dispute handling.

Later versions can add:

- Contribution rights.
- Shared ownership rules.
- Legal wrapper templates.
- Originality and rights enforcement.
- Patent provenance.
- Business spinout rules.
- Non-withholding agreements.
- Network-level governance.
- Release switch rules.
- Transparent Protheus Labs origin allocation.

## Release Switch

At critical mass, Storm may need to grow beyond control by any single organization.

The release switch should separate two ideas:

- Origin value: Protheus Labs may receive transparent ongoing value for originating and releasing Storm.
- Control: Protheus Labs should not retain hidden unilateral authority over network rules, value routing, project access, or contribution rights after release.

The system should be designed so permanent origin value does not become permanent platform domination.
