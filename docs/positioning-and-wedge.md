# Positioning And First Wedge

Storm has a large internal ambition, but the first public product should be narrow enough that users immediately understand what they can do.

## Internal Platform Thesis

Storm is a coordination, attribution, and value-routing layer for crowdsourced work.

It replaces pieces of the corporate intermediary bundle with software-mediated coordination, task decomposition, accountable review, reputation, provenance, dispute handling, and transparent payment routing.

The long-term product is not a freelance marketplace. It is a system for organizing useful work without requiring a company to own coordination, trust, customer access, and value allocation.

## Public First Wedge

The first market-facing wedge should be Story Forge.

Publicly, the first product should be framed as:

> Story Forge helps creators turn story ideas into paid, claimable, reviewable creative tasks that distributed contributors can complete together.

That wedge is concrete, emotionally legible, and close to Storm's original creative-seed thesis. It also avoids making the first user promise sound like an abstract attempt to rebuild the economy.

## Strategic Analogy

A useful shorthand:

> Storm is GitHub for creative and project work beyond code, with built-in task decomposition, review, attribution, provenance, and value routing.

The analogy should be used carefully. Storm is not just version control and is not limited to open-source-style projects. The important lesson from GitHub is that open contribution needs lineage, review, forks, merges, reputation, and durable attribution.

## What Not To Lead With

Early public positioning should not lead with:

- A general replacement for corporations.
- A new economic system.
- A broad crowdsourced work marketplace.
- Residual compensation for every idea.
- AI training compensation as the first product.
- Fully automated task decomposition.
- Real-world bounties, inventions, or regulated work.

Those ideas remain part of the internal roadmap and long-term thesis, but they are too broad for the first product promise.

## What Story Forge Proves

Story Forge should prove the Storm primitives under the hood:

- A project can be decomposed into small tasks.
- Contributors can claim and complete bounded work.
- Reviewers can approve, reject, or request changes against visible criteria.
- Accepted work can become part of a shared artifact.
- Contributors and reviewers can build reputation from verified outcomes.
- The value ledger can show how money and credit moved.
- Provenance can survive iteration, edits, forks, and later reuse.
- Disputes can be handled without collapsing the whole project.

If Story Forge works, Storm has evidence that the model can expand into other applets.

## Product Spine

The product spine remains:

- Projects.
- Tasks.
- Contributors.
- Reviewers.
- Reputation.
- Value ledger.
- Contribution value policy.

Story Forge should make these primitives visible through a story-specific interface rather than exposing abstract infrastructure to first users.

## Moat

Storm's durable advantage should come from compounding coordination data and trust records:

- Contribution provenance.
- Reputation from verified outcomes.
- Task decomposition patterns.
- Review accuracy history.
- Transparent value ledgers.
- Fork and merge lineage for non-code work.
- AI-training provenance and source attribution.
- Applet-specific review rubrics and task templates.

These are hard to copy because they depend on real work, repeated review, trusted history, and participant behavior over time.

## Primary Risks

The biggest risks are quality, dispute complexity, payout fairness, and anti-gaming.

Storm must avoid pretending that software alone solves subjective review. The product has to make review criteria visible, make reviewer behavior accountable, preserve evidence, and give disputes a bounded path.

## Expansion Path

Recommended expansion order:

1. Story Forge: story projects, creative tasks, editorial review, canon, artifact export.
2. Code Forge or open-source bounties: issue-like work with clearer acceptance criteria and existing fork/merge culture.
3. Research, design, education, community media, or game narrative applets.
4. Physical inventions and real-world bounties only after stronger rights, safety, compliance, and dispute systems exist.

The public wedge should stay narrow until the underlying primitives have enough evidence to carry the broader Storm promise.
